PRO
CHOOSING THE COCONUT MILK
Full-fat coconut milk only. Chaokoh or Aroy-D are the gold standard brands. If the can sounds sloshy when you shake it, the cream-to-water ratio is wrong. Buy another brand. The fat content is what creates a rich sauce rather than a watery one.
CHEF'S METHOD
CRACKING THE PASTE
Frying the paste until the oil visibly separates from the dry paste (called 'cracking') is the most critical technical step. Uncracked paste tastes raw and harsh. Takes 4–6 minutes on medium-high heat — the paste will colour and the kitchen will smell incredible. Be patient.
PRO
BREAKING THE COCONUT MILK
In Thai cooking, 'breaking' coconut milk intentionally is a technique: fry the paste in coconut cream until the fat separates and forms orange pools on the surface. This fat carries the spice compounds into the finished sauce. This is different from accidentally splitting a sauce.
SHORTCUT
USING STORE-BOUGHT PASTE
Mae Ploy Massaman paste is genuinely excellent and saves 45 minutes of paste-making. Use 3 heaped tablespoons per 1 kg of meat. The flavour gap between homemade and Mae Ploy is much smaller than you'd expect — probably 85% as good.
TIMING
DAY-AHEAD ADVANTAGE
Massaman is measurably better the next day. The overnight rest allows: the beef to fully absorb the sauce, the spices to bloom further, and the fat to rise and be easily removed if desired. If you can plan ahead, make it the day before serving.
VARIATION
THE BANANA VARIATION
Some Southern Thai cooks add a small piece of ripe banana to the curry in the last 10 minutes. It melts completely and adds a natural sweetness that's more floral than palm sugar. Strange but genuinely excellent. Try it once.
COMMUNITY TIP
BEEF SELECTION
Beef chuck (not brisket, not sirloin) for the right result. Chuck has the collagen content that converts to gelatin during braising, creating a thick, luxurious sauce. Sirloin will toughen and be disappointing. Chuck is also cheaper. Win-win.
