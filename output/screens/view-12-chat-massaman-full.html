Excellent choice — Massaman is one of the most complex and rewarding Thai curries. It has Persian and Indian influences from the old spice trade routes, which is why it contains warm spices like cardamom and cinnamon that you rarely see in other Thai dishes.

Here's the full recipe from Pailin (Hot Thai Kitchen):

Pailin's Tip
Toast the whole spices dry until fragrant, then let cool before grinding. This activates their oils and makes a huge difference to the depth of flavour.
Massaman Curry Step 1 / 10
In a large heavy pot over medium heat, add 2 tbsp oil. When hot, fry the Massaman curry paste for 3–4 minutes until very fragrant and the oil starts to separate. This is called 'cracking the paste'.
⏱ 3–4 min
Massaman Curry Step 2 / 10
Add 1 can of coconut milk and stir to incorporate the paste. Increase heat and cook for 5 minutes until the coconut milk reduces and the oil separates again — you'll see orange pools forming on the surface.
⏱ 5 min This separation is called 'breaking the coconut milk'
The oil separated but it looks a bit orange — is that normal?

Yes, perfectly normal! That orange oil is the curry paste aromatics infusing into the coconut fat. It's exactly what you want — that's where all the flavour lives. Pailin calls this the 'broken' stage.

Keep going — you're right on track 🙌

Massaman Curry Step 3 / 10
Add the beef chuck and toss to coat in the paste. Sear for 5–6 minutes, turning occasionally, until browned on all sides. Don't rush this — the Maillard reaction here builds deep savoury notes.
⏱ 5–6 min
💡 Work in batches if your pot isn't large enough — crowding prevents browning
Massaman Curry Step 4 / 10
Pour in the second can of coconut milk and 2 cups of water. Add cinnamon stick, cardamom pods, and bay leaves. Bring to a simmer, then reduce heat to low.
How do I know when the beef is done?

Beef chuck is done when it easily pulls apart with two forks — typically after 1.5–2 hours of slow simmering. You're looking for that melt-in-your-mouth texture, not just 'cooked through'.

The collagen in chuck needs time to convert to gelatin, which is what makes the sauce luxuriously thick and glossy. Rushing this step is the most common mistake.

Massaman Curry Step 5 / 10
Add potatoes and onions after 1 hour of simmering. They need 30–40 min more. Check the beef — it should be tender but not yet falling apart.
⏱ Add after 1h of beef simmering
Chef's Notes
⏰
Timing
Make this the day before serving — like all braises, Massaman is significantly better on day 2 as the flavours meld overnight.
🥥
Pro Tip
Use fresh coconut milk if available (find at Asian grocers) — the flavour is incomparably richer than canned. Shake canned milk well before opening.
🌶️
Variation
Add prik khing (dried shrimp paste) to the paste for an even more authentic, deeper flavour profile.

Continuing with steps 6 and 7:

6
Season to taste with fish sauce (salty), palm sugar (sweet), and tamarind (sour). The balance should be gently sweet-sour-salty-spicy with the spice aromatic rather than hot.
7
Add peanuts 10 minutes before serving. They should retain some texture — don't add them too early or they'll become soggy.

Massaman is done when the beef is completely tender, the sauce has thickened around the potatoes, and you can't stop taste-testing it.

Serve over jasmine rice with extra peanuts, fresh coriander, and lime wedges on the side. The sauce is incredible for dipping crusty bread too.
